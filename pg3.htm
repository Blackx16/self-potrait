Tokyo Tower

Tokyo Tower is a communications and observation tower in the Shiba-koen district of Minato, Tokyo, Japan. At 332.9 meters,
it is the second-tallest structure in Japan and the tallest free-standing steel tower in the world.
Tokyo Towerwas built in 1958 as a symbol of Japan's post-war reconstruction and economic development.
It is one of the most popular tourist attractions in Tokyo, and it offers stunning views of the city and the surrounding area.

What to see and do at Tokyo Tower?
 Observation decks: Tokyo Tower has two observation decks, the Main Deck and the Top Deck. The Main Deck is located at a height of 150 meters, and it offers panoramic views of Tokyo.The Top Deck is located at a height of 250 meters, and it offers even more breathtaking views.
FootTown: FootTown is a complex of shops and restaurants located at the base of Tokyo Tower. It is a great place to
grab a bite to eat or do some shopping before or after your visit to the tower.
Events : Tokyo Tower hosts a variety of events throughout the year, including concerts, exhibitions, and seasonal light-up displays.

Tokyo Skytree

The Tokyo Skytree is the tallest tower in Japan and the second-tallest structure in the world, after the Burj Khalifa in Dubai.
It is a popular tourist destination, offering stunning views of Tokyo and its surroundings. The Tokyo Skytree is located in the Sumida district of Tokyo,and is easily accessible by train or bus. The tower is open to the public from 10:00am to 9:00pm.

There are two observation decks on the Tokyo Skytree:
the Tembo Deck and the Tembo Galleria.

The Tembo Deck is located at 350 meters, and offers 360-degree views of Tokyo. The Tembo Galleria is located at 450 meters,
and offers even more spectacular views. In addition to the observation decks,The Tokyo Skytree also has a number of other attractions, including a museum, a restaurant, and a gift shop. There is also a special tour of the tower's broadcasting
equipment available. If you are visiting Tokyo, the Tokyo Skytree is a must-see attraction. The tower offers stunning
views of the city, and there is something for everyone to enjoy.

Meiji Jingu

Meiji Jingu is a Shinto shrine in Shibuya, Tokyo, that is dedicated to the deified spirits of Emperor Meiji and his wife,
Empress Shōken. The shrine does not contain the emperor's grave, which is located at Fushimi-momoyama, south of Kyoto.
Meiji Jingu is one of the most popular tourist destinations in Tokyo, and is known for its beautiful forest setting and its many traditional Shinto rituals. Visitors can take part in activities such as making offerings at the main hall, buying charms
and amulets, or writing out their wishes on an ema. The shrine is also a popular spot for weddings, and visitors can often see traditional Shinto weddings taking place on the grounds.

What things you can see and do at Meiji Jingu?

  • Visit the main hall and make an offering.
  • Buy a charm or amulet from one of the many stalls on the grounds.
  • Write out your wish on an ema and hang it on the wishing tree.
  • Take a walk through the forest and enjoy the peace and quiet.
  • Visit the Meiji Jingu Museum to learn more about the history of the shrine and the lives of Emperor Meiji and Empress Shōken.
  • Attend a traditional Shinto wedding ceremony (if you are lucky enough to be visiting on the day of a wedding).
Sensō-ji

Sensō-ji (浅草寺), Tokyo's oldest Buddhist temple, dating back to 628 AD, is not just a historical marvel
but a vibrant cultural hub. Nestled in the heart of Asakusa, it reverberates with spiritual energy and echoes
of ancient traditions. While the main hall enshrines the revered Kannon statue, the towering five-story
pagoda offers panoramic views of the temple complex. Nakamise shopping street, adorned with
traditional stalls, immerses visitors in Japan's rich heritage. Beyond its religious significance, Sensō-ji hosts
lively festivals, like the renowned Sanja Matsuri, captivating travelers with its lively processions and vibrant celebrations. Accessible via Asakusa Station, this temple is a must-visit, blending spirituality,
history, and cultural allure in the bustling metropolis of Tokyo and seasonal light-up displays. It is 55
meters tall, and it offers stunning views of the temple grounds and the surrounding area.

Tokyo National Museum

The Tokyo National Museum (TNM) nestled in Ueno Park, stands as Japan's largest and oldest
museum, showcasing an extensive array of Japanese and Asian art. Boasting over 100 national treasures, the museum's six galleries offer a profound journey into diverse cultural realms.
The Japanese Gallery (Honkan) displays exquisite paintings, sculptures, ceramics, and lacquerware,
while the Japanese Archaeology and Special Exhibition Gallery (Heiseikan) explores Japan's
archaeological wonders and hosts diverse exhibitions. The Asian Gallery (Toyokan) houses art from China,
Korea, India, Southeast Asia, and Central Asia, captivating visitors with its richness.
The Gallery of Horyuji Treasures showcases Buddhist art from the UNESCO World Heritage Site, Horyuji Temple, dating back to the 7th century. Additionally, the museum features the Kuroda Memorial Hall,
exhibiting Western paintings, and the Hyokeikan, displaying Japanese crafts. TNM's collection includes
the revered Horyuji Treasures, over 100 national treasures such as the Tamamushi Shrine and the
Twelve Divine Generals, and an extensive Asian art assortment. Alongside these treasures, the museum offers enriching educational programs, making it a quintessential destination for art enthusiasts and cultural explorers.
